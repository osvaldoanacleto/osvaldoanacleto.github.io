Current research

At the Roslin Institute I am developing novel statistical models, Bayesian computational algorithms and experimental designs to study the role of genetic variation and other non-genetic factors in the spread of infectious diseases in animal populations.

Most of my current research aims to estimate the genetic heterogeneity in the propensity of infected individuals to transmit diseases. Variation in this host infectivity leads to the superspreading phenomenon, where a minority of highly infected hosts are responsible for transmitting the majority of infections. To date it is not known to what extent host infectivity and superspreading are genetically controlled.

I am currently involved in interdisciplinary projects with researchers based in Norway, Spain, Brazil and the United States to design, carry out and analyse disease experiments in fish and livestock, and to apply our novel methods to the resulting data. Preliminary analyses of data available so far show, for the very first time, that genetics does indeed contribute to variation in infectivity and therefore affects the spread of diseases.
